# Ortho94

The orthography released in ROC year 94

## Amis

The letter `f` can be pronounced as /b/, /v/, or /f/, depending on dialect. Not much information is provided, beyond that /b/ is more common in the north and /f/ is more common in the south. We set Nanshi Amis to use /b/ and all others to use /f/.

The letter `d` similarly has three pronunciations (which use non-standard characters that were hard to copy into this document). Not much information is provided, beyond that /d/ is more common in the north and /ɬ/ is more common in the south. We set Nanshi Amis to use /d/ and all others to use /ɬ/.

## Atayal

Does not use `_` as a vowel, which was added in Ortho113. This cannot be easily recovered.

## Paiwan

No notes

## Bunun

They note that `-` is often used as a syllable separator. This is removed when converting to IPA. Later in Ortho113, it is noted that `-` was sometimes used instead of ''' for glottal stops. Which we can't do much about easily.  

There are nine diphthongs in the five Bunun dialects: ai, au, ia, iu, ua, ui, aa, uu, ii. However, the orthography doesn't say what phonemes are involved, so we treat them (for now) as if they are not dipthongs. 

## Puyuma

The /ts/ sound represented by the letter 'c' appears only in Jianhu; in other dialects, the letter 'c' is only used in loanwords, for example, in the word ciwcika ‘cross’. As a result, for simplicity, we do not list `c` as available for the other dialects.

### Conversion

Conversion is straightforward. Ortho113 changed some pronunciations and which dialects have which characters, but that will not cause problems.

However, Ortho113 added `ll`. There's no obvious way to add that in retroactively. 

## Rukai

Maolin uses `_` to distinguish consonant clusters like /dr/. This should be handled in the table.

## Saisiyat

The voiceless dental fricative /s/ and the voiced dental fricative /z/ in the Da'ai and Donghe dialects represent two different phonetic values. Because these are not officially recognized dialects, we abuse the ~ notation.

### Conversion

No issues.

## Tsou

Ortho94 included two additional dialects (Kanakanafu, Shaalua) which were deleted in Ortho113. We don't record them in the table for the time being.

In Tsou writing, the central high labial vowel [ɨ] is primarily written as 'ʉ', except in cases where the letter cannot be typed, in which case it is replaced by 'x'.

## Yami

No notes.

### Conversion

No issues

## Kavalan

No notes.

### Conversion

No issues

## Sakizaya

The notes say: "The letter 'f' represents three pronunciations depending on the region: [b, v, f]. Generally speaking, the closer to a northern dialect, the more likely 'f' is to represent the [b] sound; the closer to a southern dialect, the more likely it is to represent the [f] sound." However, it looks like this was later standardized to /b/, so for simplicity, we use that phoneme only.

The notes say: "The letter 'd' represents three pronunciations depending on the region: [d, ð, ɬ]. Generally speaking, the closer to a northern dialect, the more likely 'd' is to represent the [d] sound; the closer to a southern dialect, the more likely it is to represent the [ɬ] sound." Again, this appears to have then been standardized to /d/, so we use that.

# Ortho113

Revision to Ortho94, released in ROC year 113

## Amis

THe notes from Ortho94 still apply.

### Nanshi

There is now a separate orthography for Nanshi Amis, since they were unable to agree with the others.

## Atayal

Similar to Ortho94, though some phonemes have been refined. 

Notes that `n_g` is used to distinguish `n`+`g` from `ng`. This is handled by adding an orthographic form `n_g` in the chart.

By itself, `_` can be used for /ə/. To handle this, `_` is entered into the chart after `n_g`. The code does transformations in order from the top of the chart, so this ensures `n_g` will be handled before remaining `_` are handled.

The mid-vowels [e] and [o] vary within different Atayal language sub-species. For example, most tribes of the Saikaolik Atayal language use the written characters "e" and "o", but in some areas, such as Taoshan Village in Wufeng Township, Hsinchu County, the Saikaolik Atayal native vocabulary does not use the written characters "e" and "o".

## Paiwan

Notes that in Majia Township and some tribes in Taiwu Township, there is a phenomenon of replacing [s] with [h], for example, [tisun].

Also notes that `o` continues to be used in spelling some loanwords and function words, though no such phoneme exists distinct from /u/.

## Bunun

Similar to the Ortho94, but with more special spelling rules involving `c` and `s`.

The documentation states that `-` used to represent a glottal stop, but I haven't seen any such orthographies yet. Now it is used to specify syllable boundaries.

## Puyuma

No additional notes beyond what is in Ortho94.

## Rukai

In Wanshan Rukai, if a word contains the ancient alveolar trill [r] which has been transformed into the voiceless glottal fricative [h] through phonetic variation, it is not mandatory for contemporary users to pronounce this sound. In formal teaching materials, it can be presented side-by-side or as an annotation; in formal situations concerning rights, both methods are correct.

A major change from Ortho94 is that many cases where a letter was marked as not used in a particular dialect, it is now marked as used for loan words. In cases where every dialect uses the letter as either part of the native orthography or for loan words, we include it in the table.

## Saisiyat

`:` has complex uses and should be left in place. This renders the IPA nonstandard.

## Tsou

In Tsou writing, the central high labial vowel [ɨ] is primarily written as 'ʉ', except in cases where the letter cannot be typed, in which case it is replaced by 'x'.

## Yami

The `'` was dropped from the official orthography but still retained for use. So we leave it in the table.

The letter v was originally a voiced labiodental fricative [v]. Younger generations have gradually replaced it with the voiceless labiodental fricative [f], hence its IPA symbol on the consonant table is [f]. However, the [v] sound is still heard in the Lang Island tribe.

For loanwords, `s` can be pronounced /s/.

## Thao

There are 9 dipthongs, but their pronunciation is not explained.

## Kavalan

The pronunciation of the letter z varies regionally. In Hualien, it is a voiced alveolar fricative [z], while in Taitung, it is primarily a trilled alveolar [r].

According to the *Kavalan Dictionary* co-authored by Li Rengui and Tu Tianzhi (2006:3), when *d* appears before a vowel, it is pronounced as either a voiced alveolar plosive [d] or a voiced interdental fricative [ð]. If it appears at the end of a word, it is pronounced as a voiced lateral alveolar fricative [ɮ].

When the high front vowel [i] is in the same syllable as either a voiced uvular fricative [ʁ] or a voiceless uvular plosive [q], it is consistently pronounced as the front mid vowel [e]. Since no minimal pair of [i] and [e] has been found, and the occurrence of [i] and [e] can be predicted according to phonological rules, [i] and [e] are considered appositives, and the letter *i* is used to represent both vowels.

When the high back vowel [u] is in the same syllable as the voiced uvular fricative [ʁ] or the voiceless uvular stop [q], it is always pronounced as the back mid vowel [o]. Since no minimal pair of [u] and [o] has been found, and the occurrence of [u] and [o] can be predicted according to phonological rules, [u] and [o] are considered appositives and uniformly represented by the letter u.


## Truku

When the letter g appears at the end of a word, if it is preceded by the vowel i, it glides to [j]. If it is preceded by the vowel a or u, it glides to [w].

Currently, the function of glottal stops is expressed by repeatedly writing the central vowel ee; therefore, it has been decided not to add glottal stop letters, e.g., mkeekan (fight), beeba (sore).

High vowels [i] and [u] before and after the letters h and q cause the vowels to drop, becoming the central vowels [e] and [o] respectively. This is not included in the table.

## Seediq

Wasn't sure what was intended by this note, so it is not handled: "The final diphthong ay [aj] in Dudas and Delugh Seediq corresponds to the front mid-vowel e [e] in Degudaya Seediq, and the final diphthong aw [aw] in Dudas and Delugh Seediq corresponds to the back mid-vowel o [o] in Degudaya Seediq." Sounds like they are describing phonemic changes between the dialects, not spelling rules.

## Sakizaya

From the notes: In Sakihaya, the use of the letter 'z' sometimes corresponds to the letter 'd'. For example, zazan ("road"), izaw ("have/be there"), tayza ("go"), and mahiza ("like this") correspond to dadan ("road"), idaw ("have/be there"), tayda ("go"), and mahida ("like this"), respectively.

`f` has been replaced with `b`.

`r` is now `l`

## Saaroa

Main difference appears to be that `e` was dropped, along with the corresponding phoneme. Instead, there is `ʉ`, representing /ɨ/.

## Kanakanavu

The `r` now covers both /r/ and /ɾ/ (the `l` was dropped).

There is `ʉ`, representing /ɨ/.

The `e` now only has an /e/ sound (the /ə/ is no longer indicated).
